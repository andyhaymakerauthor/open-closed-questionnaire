Weights & Measures:
where do you stand on open vs closed?

This instrument separates two things that usually get tangled: what you believe is true (empirical credences, Part I) and what you care about (value weights, Part II). Part III shows how the two couple into pulls toward each pole, which beliefs are load-bearing, and what single change of mind would flip your lean. Part IV is where you write the stance in your own words. The needle above follows you the whole way down.

The decision space is not binary. Between "closed everything" and "open everything" sit real, defensible positions: structured researcher access, staged release, open-below-a-capability-threshold, open-weights-but-closed-training-data. The beam lands you in a neighborhood, not on a verdict. Also: your stance may legitimately differ by role — what you run personally, what you advocate publicly, and what you build on are three different questions. Part IV treats them separately. One more degree of freedom: weights are not the only control point. Compute and hardware are physical and governable even when software distribution controls fail — so "open weights" and "ungoverned" can be decoupled, which softens several tradeoffs below.
PART I

Empirical credences

For each claim, set your credence that it is true — 0 means almost certainly false, 100 almost certainly true. These are questions about the world, not about what you want. Each card notes what would move the estimate, because a credence you can't imagine updating isn't a credence.

PART II

Value weights

How much does each of these matter to you, independent of which pole serves it? Resist the urge to set everything high — the instrument only becomes informative when weights differ. A useful prompt: which of these would you trade away first if forced?

R · RISK ATTITUDE — decision theory, not probability 1.00×
How much should a catastrophe-class outcome count, relative to its probability-weighted average?
The instrument otherwise adds pulls linearly — risk-neutral expected value, which is itself a contested stance — so this dial makes that assumption yours to set. It multiplies every catastrophe-class pull on both sides equally (marked · R in the wiring): containment, recall, war-making containment, denial, dampened competition, and control custody toward closed; defense & plural ecology, demilitarized structure, defusing the race, and anti-singleton plurality toward open. At 1× nothing changes. Above 1×, irreversible mass-casualty outcomes count for more than their expected value. Below 1×, you discount speculative tail arguments. Your probability estimates live in Part I — this dial is about your attitude toward a given probability.

The wiring

Each value exerts a pull toward one pole, conditional on your beliefs. Misuse-prevention pulls closed only to the degree you believe open weights add marginal capability (B1), that closure actually withholds it (low B2), and that closure buys more than a delay (low B7). The coupling formulas below are this instrument's proposal, shown on purpose: if one feels wrong, that disagreement is itself a finding — note it in Part IV. B and V symbols are your sliders, scaled 0–1. Four beliefs are deliberately two-sided — B14 (race defusal), B16 (surveillance vector), B17 (footprint shift), and B19 (open usability) decide which pole a value pulls toward, not just how hard. And several values are contested between the poles: misuse, demilitarization, race stability, human control, surveillance, and footprint, and everyday access each appear on both sides of the table, with your beliefs adjudicating. Pulls marked · R are catastrophe-class and scaled by the risk-attitude dial at the end of Part II — applied to both sides equally, so it sharpens the contest rather than picking a winner.

One structural honesty note: the closed side’s biggest pulls are conjunctions — several uncertain beliefs multiplied together — while several open pulls serve their values directly. A consequence is that with every slider at 50, the beam already leans open: that starting lean reflects the wiring’s structure, not a measurement of you. If you think it bakes in a bias, that is a legitimate objection — log it under disagreements in Part IV.

Sensitivity — which inputs are load-bearing?

Each bar shows where the needle would travel if that one input swept from 0 to 100, everything else held fixed. A bar that crosses the center line means that input, by itself, could flip your lean — whether any bar does depends on how far your current configuration sits from the balance point. When nothing crosses, your lean is robust to any single change of mind, and the longest bars still tell you which inputs the position leans on hardest — those are the ones worth researching before publishing anything.

    PART IV

    Articulation

    The instrument stops here; you take over. Write the stance in sentences. A position you can't state with its strongest counterargument attached isn't ready to be advocated.

    Is your position conditional on capability? Name the threshold at which it would change — a benchmark, a demonstrated uplift, an incident class. "Open until X" is a stance; "open, and I'll know the limit when I see it" is not.

    What runs on your own hardware, what runs through APIs, and why? Where does the 18-month local-hardware bet change your plans?

    What do you argue for in public, in one paragraph? Include the strongest objection you will steelman in the same breath — the readers you most want to reach will supply it anyway.

    If you build products, games, or platforms on AI: what powers them, and is that choice legible to your users? Infrastructure is part of the statement.

    Where do the coupling formulas in Part III misrepresent how you think the world works? These notes are the seed of the version of this instrument you'd build yourself.
